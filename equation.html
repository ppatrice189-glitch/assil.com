Quadratic Equations & Inequalities

A quadratic polynomial is an expression of the form \( ax^2 + bx + c \), where \( a \), \( b \), and \( c \) are real constants. Generally, we assume \( a \neq 0 \), as \( a = 0 \) would simplify the expression to \( bx + c \), which belongs to linear equations.

Proposition 1: Sign of the Polynomial

Let \( a, b, c \in \mathbb{R} \) with \( a \neq 0 \). Consider the quadratic polynomial \( P(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \).

  • If \( b^2 - 4ac < 0 \), then \( P(x) \) has the sign of \( a \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) and never equals zero.
  • If \( b^2 - 4ac = 0 \), then \( P(x) \) has the sign of \( a \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \) and equals zero at \( x = \frac{-b}{2a} \).
  • If \( b^2 - 4ac > 0 \), then \( P(x) \) has two distinct roots \( x_1 \) and \( x_2 \), and its sign is the same as \( a \) for \( x \in (-\infty, x_1) \cup (x_2, \infty) \), and opposite to \( a \) for \( x \in (x_1, x_2) \).

Proposition 2: Roots of the Polynomial

When the discriminant is strictly positive, the roots \( x_1 \) and \( x_2 \) of the quadratic polynomial \( P(x) \) are given explicitly by the quadratic formula:

\[ x_1 = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}, \quad x_2 = \frac{-b + \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \]

Proposition 3: Interpretation of the Discriminant

Based on the value of the discriminant \( \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \):

  • If \( \Delta < 0 \), the polynomial has no real roots, meaning the parabola never crosses the x-axis.
  • If \( \Delta = 0 \), the polynomial has exactly one real root, meaning the parabola touches the x-axis tangentially at its vertex.
  • If \( \Delta > 0 \), the polynomial has two distinct real roots, meaning the parabola crosses the x-axis at two distinct points.
